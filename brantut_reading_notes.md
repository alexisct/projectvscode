# Nicolas Brantut Reading Notes

## Status

These notes summarize the first deep-reading pass through the most relevant papers for:

- fault stability
- induced seismicity

The emphasis here is on extracting mechanisms and synthesis-ready findings, not reproducing every detail of each paper.

## Core Notes

### 2018 - Fault Reactivation by Fluid Injection: Controls From Stress State and Injection Rate

- DOI: `10.1029/2018GL080470`
- Type: laboratory experiment with interpretation in terms of rupture initiation criteria
- Relevance: very high
- Themes: `induced-seismicity`, `fault-stability`, `fluids`, `lab-model-iteration`

Main question:

- How do stress state and injection rate control fault reactivation during fluid injection?

Approach:

- Saw-cut Westerly granite sample under triaxial stress conditions
- Fluid injected through a borehole directly connected to the fault
- Elastic wave velocity monitored along strike to infer pressure heterogeneity

Main findings:

- The borehole pressure required to trigger reactivation increases with injection rate.
- High injection rates create strong along-fault pore-pressure heterogeneity.
- Under these conditions, reactivation departs from simple Coulomb/effective-stress expectations.
- Reactivation is better viewed as a nonlocal rupture-initiation problem rather than a purely local pressure-threshold problem.

Implications:

- This is one of the clearest Brantut-linked papers for induced seismicity.
- It argues that injection protocol matters, not just total pressure increase.
- It also helps explain why seismicity can continue after injection if local overpressures and undrained zones persist or migrate.

Why it matters for the overall review:

- It establishes a recurring Brantut theme: hydraulic state is spatially heterogeneous, and fault slip cannot always be predicted from a single local pore-pressure measurement.

### 2019 - Stability of Pulse-Like Earthquake Ruptures

- DOI: `10.1029/2019JB017926`
- Type: theoretical and numerical modelling
- Relevance: very high
- Themes: `fault-stability`, `modelling`, `rupture`

Main question:

- Are steady-state pulse-like ruptures stable, and what does their stability imply for earthquake propagation?

Approach:

- Theoretical analysis plus elastodynamic simulations
- Thermal pressurization used as the representative weakening mechanism
- Slip, slip-rate, and pulse-width perturbations tracked under small stress perturbations

Main findings:

- Steady-state pulses driven by thermal pressurization are unstable.
- Negative perturbations cause rupture arrest.
- Positive perturbations cause growth into expanding pulses or crack-like ruptures, depending on background stress.
- Pulse instability helps define the minimum stress conditions for sustained rupture.

Implications:

- Brantut treats fault stability here as a dynamic rupture-selection problem, not just a static friction problem.
- The paper suggests that pulse-like rupture is central for understanding when faults can sustain seismic slip.
- It also implies that rupture complexity can arise spontaneously even on otherwise homogeneous faults.

Why it matters for the overall review:

- This is a key modelling paper for the executive summary because it shows Brantut using theory to redefine fault “strength” in dynamic terms.

### 2019 - Rupture Energetics in Crustal Rock From Laboratory-Scale Seismic Tomography

- DOI: `10.1029/2019GL083040`
- Type: laboratory experiment plus inversion/tomography and energetic interpretation
- Relevance: high
- Themes: `lab-model-iteration`, `rupture`, `modelling`

Main question:

- How much rupture energy is dissipated off-fault, and how does off-fault damage evolve during rupture?

Approach:

- Laboratory rock-fracture experiment
- Time-resolved 3-D seismic tomography during rupture
- Estimation of the partition between total fracture energy and off-fault dissipation

Main findings:

- Off-fault damage contributes only a minority of the total energy budget, but it is not negligible.
- The paper estimates off-fault dissipation at about 3% of total energy and about 10% of fracture energy.
- Off-fault damage significantly alters elastic properties near the fault early in rupture.

Implications:

- Off-fault damage may matter less for total energy balance than for how rupture evolves afterward.
- This is important for modelling because it provides a laboratory benchmark for dynamic rupture models.

Why it matters for the overall review:

- This is a strong example of Brantut’s lab-to-model loop: measure evolving structure in the lab, then use those observations to constrain rupture models.

### 2021 - Fault Friction During Simulated Seismic Slip Pulses

- DOI: `10.1029/2021JB022149`
- Type: laboratory friction experiments interpreted with constitutive models and elastodynamic compatibility tests
- Relevance: very high
- Themes: `friction`, `modelling`, `lab-model-iteration`, `rupture`

Main question:

- Is laboratory fault strength during realistic, non-constant earthquake slip histories compatible with theoretical rupture kinematics?

Approach:

- High-velocity friction experiments using realistic earthquake-like slip histories
- Comparison of observed strength evolution against flash-heating and viscous-creep models
- Comparison against stress histories expected from a steady-state slip-pulse model

Main findings:

- Strength evolution depends strongly on slip history, not just instantaneous slip rate.
- Short-timescale weakening is consistent with flash heating.
- Longer-timescale behavior is better described by viscous creep.
- Experimental strength evolution is only partly compatible with the tested elastodynamic slip-pulse history.
- The mismatch suggests real faults may accelerate and decelerate faster than the imposed experimental history.

Implications:

- This paper makes Brantut’s lab/model iteration especially explicit.
- It shows him using experiments not just to document weakening, but to test whether proposed earthquake source-time histories are physically plausible.

Why it matters for the overall review:

- This is one of the best papers for showing how Brantut moves back and forth between controlled experiments and dynamic rupture theory.

### 2021 - Dilatancy Toughening of Shear Cracks and Implications for Slow Rupture Propagation

- DOI: `10.1029/2021JB022239`
- Type: theoretical modelling grounded in laboratory-scale parameters
- Relevance: very high
- Themes: `fault-stability`, `modelling`, `fluids`, `lab-model-iteration`

Main question:

- How does fault-slip dilatancy change rupture propagation, especially for slow ruptures?

Approach:

- Steadily propagating shear-crack model
- Slip weakening, slip-dependent dilation, and fluid flow are all coupled

Main findings:

- Dilatancy lowers pore pressure near the rupture tip and raises frictional strength.
- This acts as a toughness increase for rupture growth.
- At higher rupture speed relative to hydraulic diffusion, the tip behaves more undrained and therefore stronger.
- Thermal pressurization can partly offset dilatant strengthening.
- The resulting mechanics are consistent with observed slow-slip behavior when using plausible lab-constrained parameters.

Implications:

- Fault stability is strongly controlled by hydro-mechanical coupling at the rupture tip.
- Slow rupture is not just a weak version of fast rupture; it may be stabilized by fluid-diffusion timescales and dilatancy.

Why it matters for the overall review:

- This paper is central to Brantut’s modelling contribution and to the lab-informed interpretation of slow rupture phenomena.

### 2023 - Rupture and Afterslip Controlled by Spontaneous Local Fluid Flow in Crustal Rock

- DOI: `10.1029/2023JB027534`
- Type: laboratory experiment with mechanistic interpretation and upscaling argument
- Relevance: very high
- Themes: `fault-stability`, `fluids`, `lab-model-iteration`, `afterslip`

Main question:

- How are rupture and immediate post-rupture slip affected by local pore-fluid flow?

Approach:

- Triaxial experiments on fluid-saturated intact rock
- Acoustic-emission locations combined with pore-pressure and mechanical data

Main findings:

- Faulting occurs in two phases.
- Phase 1 is rupture growth with strong dilatancy and local pore-pressure drop.
- Phase 2 is afterslip on the newly formed fault, driven by fluid recharge into dilated fault space.
- Dilatancy can locally stabilize rupture growth, but subsequent recharge can drive continued slip.

Implications:

- This paper is especially important because it links pore-pressure dynamics to both rupture arrest/stabilization and post-seismic slip.
- It suggests that early afterslip can arise from local recharge rather than only from large-scale external fluid migration.

Why it matters for the overall review:

- It captures one of Brantut’s clearest recurring ideas: fluids do not simply weaken faults monotonically; they can first stabilize and then re-enable slip, depending on timing and geometry.

### 2024 - Opposite Variations for Pore Pressure on and off the Fault During Simulated Earthquakes in the Laboratory

- DOI: `10.1029/2024JB028829`
- Type: laboratory experiment plus simplified analytical model
- Relevance: very high
- Themes: `fluids`, `fault-stability`, `induced-seismicity`, `lab-model-iteration`

Main question:

- How does pore pressure evolve simultaneously on the fault and in the surrounding wall rock during rupture?

Approach:

- Triaxial rupture experiments in intact Westerly granite
- Direct on-fault and off-fault pore-pressure measurements
- Simplified analytical scaling model for the pressure perturbations

Main findings:

- Shear failure produces opposite-signed pore-pressure changes over short distances.
- On-fault pressure drops because faulting is dilatant.
- Off-fault pressure rises because surrounding damaged rock compactifies as pre-existing dilatant cracks close.
- Off-fault pressure increase requires relatively undrained conditions and bulk porosity reduction.
- The authors argue that the off-fault rise could help trigger neighboring instabilities.

Implications:

- This is highly relevant to induced seismicity and earthquake interaction because it proposes a local fluid-mediated transfer mechanism to nearby faults.
- It strengthens the idea that the hydraulic consequences of rupture are spatially structured and mechanically important.

Why it matters for the overall review:

- This paper pushes Brantut’s fluid-fault work from “what happens on the slipping fault” to “how rupture perturbs the surrounding hydraulic field.”

### 2024 - Permeability Development During Fault Growth and Slip in Granite

- DOI: `10.1029/2024JB029057`
- Type: laboratory experiment with hydraulic monitoring during progressive fault growth
- Relevance: high
- Themes: `fluids`, `fault-stability`, `lab-model-iteration`, `permeability`

Main question:

- When during fault growth does permeability change most, and how heterogeneous is the evolving permeability field?

Approach:

- Triaxial deformation experiments on intact Westerly granite
- Repeated flow tests at different stages of deformation and fault development
- Pore-pressure measurements along the prospective fault and acoustic-emission monitoring of rupture growth

Main findings:

- Average permeability increases by about two orders of magnitude before peak stress, while the fault is still not through-going.
- During post-peak fault growth, overall permeability increases much less, but local heterogeneity becomes strong.
- Local permeability variations up to about a factor of eight are associated with only partially connected fracture networks in the cohesive/process zone.
- Once the fault is fully formed and slip continues, transmissivity stays comparatively stable even though dilation continues.

Implications:

- The main hydraulic reorganization happens ahead of or during fault formation, not only after a mature fault exists.
- Fault stability in fluid-bearing rock depends on evolving transmissivity structure, not just on the existence of a final fault core.
- This provides a bridge between rupture mechanics and longer-timescale fault-zone hydrology.

Why it matters for the overall review:

- It complements the pore-pressure papers by showing where permeability structure comes from during rupture development.

### 2025 - Propagation of Fluid-Driven Aseismic Slip Fronts Along Crustal Faults

- DOI: `10.21203/rs.3.rs-6812099/v1`
- Status: preprint / in review
- Type: laboratory experiment interpreted with fracture-mechanics ideas
- Relevance: very high
- Themes: `induced-seismicity`, `fluids`, `modelling`, `lab-model-iteration`

Main question:

- During fluid injection, when does an aseismic slip front lag the pressure front and when does it outrun it?

Approach:

- Granite fault experiments under upper-crustal stress conditions
- Simultaneous tracking of pore-pressure-front propagation and aseismic slip-front propagation during injection
- Interpretation through fracture-mechanics scaling

Main findings:

- Aseismic slip lags the fluid-pressure front at low injection rate and low initial stress.
- Aseismic slip can outpace the pressure front when injection is faster or the fault is closer to failure.
- The transition is governed by a dimensionless loading or stress-injection parameter combining injection conditions and stress state.
- This directly supports fracture-mechanics predictions for fluid-driven aseismic slip.

Implications:

- This is one of the clearest links between Brantut-related lab work and induced-seismicity hazard.
- It suggests that triggered slip and potentially triggered seismicity can migrate farther than expected from pressure diffusion alone.
- It also helps explain why seismic swarms can reflect aseismic stress transfer rather than just pore-pressure diffusion.

Why it matters for the overall review:

- It connects several strands in the broader Brantut corpus: injection rate effects, fault stress state, fluid-pressure heterogeneity, and the use of mechanics models to explain propagation regimes.

## Cross-Paper Themes Emerging So Far

### 1. Fault Stability Is Dynamic and Hydro-Mechanical

Brantut’s work does not treat stability as a simple static failure threshold.

Instead, stability depends on:

- pore-pressure diffusion versus deformation rate
- local dilation and compaction
- thermal weakening and thermal pressurization
- rupture mode selection
- the timing of recharge after a dilatant event

### 2. Fluids Can Stabilize and Destabilize the Same Fault

A strong recurring theme is temporal reversal:

- during rupture growth, dilatancy lowers pore pressure and stabilizes slip
- after rupture or under different injection conditions, fluid recharge or localized overpressure can promote renewed slip or instability

This is a more nuanced picture than “higher pressure means more slip.”

### 3. Injection-Induced Seismicity Is Sensitive to Spatial Heterogeneity

The induced-seismicity-related papers consistently suggest that:

- local pressure heterogeneity matters more than a single borehole pressure threshold
- injection rate changes the drainage regime and therefore the triggering physics
- aseismic slip and recharge processes may help explain migration and persistence of seismicity
- slip fronts can propagate differently from pore-pressure fronts, especially near failure or at high injection rate

### 4. Models Are Used to Explain Mechanisms, Not Replace Experiments

Brantut’s modelling papers are tightly linked to measurable physical mechanisms:

- pulse stability and rupture mode selection
- dilatancy toughening
- thermal versus viscous controls on friction
- energetic consequences of off-fault damage

The modelling is usually there to interpret, generalize, or upscale laboratory observations.

### 5. The Lab-to-Model Loop Looks Like a Defining Feature

The recurring workflow is:

1. Build a controlled experiment that captures a physical process of faulting.
2. Measure not only stress and slip, but also acoustic, hydraulic, or microstructural variables.
3. Use theory or simplified models to isolate the controlling mechanism.
4. Upscale cautiously to natural faults and induced-seismicity problems.

## Provisional Synthesis

At this stage, the center of gravity of Brantut’s relevant work appears to be:

- fault weakening and strengthening mechanisms during rupture
- pore-pressure evolution during and after fault slip
- how rupture dynamics depend on coupled friction, fluids, and thermal effects
- how carefully designed experiments can discriminate between competing theoretical models

The strongest direct connection to induced seismicity comes from the fluid-injection and pore-pressure papers, but many of the more general rupture and dilatancy papers are indirectly important because they explain how fluid-affected faults gain or lose stability.
